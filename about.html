About BuildProposal

I built BuildProposal because I kept losing projects that should have been mine. The problem wasn't my work — it was that my proposal looked like a quote with extra words pasted around it. Clients with real budgets expect a document that shows you've thought through the project, not just priced it.

A proposal is different from a quote. A quote says "here's what it costs." A proposal says "here's why you should hire us, here's how we'll do it, here's the timeline, and here's the investment." BuildProposal gives you the structure for all of that without making you design anything.

When should you use a proposal instead of a quote? Any project over $5,000. Any client who's comparing multiple vendors. Any engagement where scope, timeline, or approach matters as much as price. If the client needs to understand what they're buying — not just how much it costs — send a proposal.

The key sections are intentional. The executive summary frames the problem before you present the solution. The scope of work breaks the project into phases with deliverables. The timeline shows you've planned the work. And the pricing table comes last — after the client is already sold on the approach.

BuildProposal is part of a small ecosystem of free tools for independent work: BuildQuotes for quick price estimates, BuildInvoice for getting paid, BuildChangeOrder for scope changes, BuildPDF for file conversion, and BuildCertificates. No account needed for any of them.
